*Produced: 2026-04-26*

## 1. Assessment of Current Free/Paid Structure

### What the free tier does

Free delivers: 1 defense response + 1 contract analysis, no card required. Access to all 21 tool types is technically granted but consumption is capped at one use each. The signup CTA reads "Run a free situation."

### Verdict: the free tier is calibrated correctly, but its value communication is weak

**Not too generous.** One response and one contract analysis is not enough for a user to get comfortable and stop upgrading. It is exactly enough to demonstrate the product's core value loop: pick a situation, get a copy-ready message in under 30 seconds. That is the moment of conviction. The free tier is designed to create it once, then stop.

**Not too restrictive.** The risk of too-restrictive free access (e.g., no response at all — just a demo) is that the user never achieves the "this works" moment inside the product. The landing page demo is good, but reading a live response to your own actual client situation is categorically more convincing. One response is the right number: enough to feel it, not enough to rely on it.

**The problem is narrative, not quantity.** The free tier is currently positioned as a sample ("try it") rather than a standing offer with a specific job: "handle your first situation for free." The current pricing page headline — "Start free. Own every situation." — contradicts itself. The first phrase implies access; the second phrase is a Pro-only promise. This creates confusion about what "free" actually delivers.

**Structural weakness:** There is no time-boxing on free usage. The 1 free response does not expire, so a user who signed up, used their one response, and resolved their situation has no urgency to return or upgrade. This reduces reactivation pressure.

### Recommendations for the free tier

- Rename the free plan internally as "Starter" or "First Situation" — both for copy and for user psychology. It is not a long-term free product; it is a trial that happens to have no card.
- Make explicit in the free CTA what the ceiling is: "Handle your first situation free — no card." This primes the user to understand the upgrade is coming, which reduces friction when it appears.
- Do not increase the free limit. One response is the right amount.
- Consider a 30-day expiry on unused free credits to drive activation velocity once email is captured.

## 2. Willingness-to-Pay Analysis for Solo Freelancers at €20/Month

### The economic argument is strong

The product marketing context already frames the correct counterargument to the "€20/month feels like a lot" objection: one enforced kill fee or recovered invoice pays for 12+ months of Pro. This is true and quantifiable.

For a freelancer billing at €75–150/hour, scope creep of even 2 extra hours — which Pushback deflects — more than covers a full year of Pro subscription. The ROI story is decisive and mathematically indisputable for any freelancer billing above approximately €2,000/year.

### The psychological barrier is the real obstacle

Willingness-to-pay for a solo freelancer at €20/month depends less on whether the product is worth it and more on:

1. **Frequency of acute need.** Freelancers who experience difficult client situations monthly will convert readily. Freelancers who have experienced one situation in six months will stall on the upgrade. The product's value is insurance-like — high when needed, invisible when not. Insurance at €20/month can feel expensive when no claim is pending.

2. **Perceived category.** If the user mentally categorizes Pushback as "AI writing tool," they compare to €20/month for Jasper or ChatGPT Plus and feel the price is high. If they categorize it as "freelance business protection," they compare to a lawyer's hourly rate (€150–300) and feel the price is trivially low. The product must win the categorization battle before the price lands.

3. **Monthly commitment friction.** "Cancel anytime" reduces anxiety but does not eliminate the psychological cost of seeing €20 on a monthly bank statement when there has been no difficult client situation this month. Annual billing reframes this.

### Verdict: €20/month is correctly positioned, but has a conversion gap

€20/month is not too high for the value delivered. It is, however, in the zone where the psychological cost of commitment exceeds the perceived immediate utility for users who are not currently mid-crisis. The solution is not to lower the price — it is to reduce the commitment friction and sharpen the ROI narrative.

## 3. Whether Usage-Based Limits Are the Right Value Metric

### The current limits

Pro: 150 defense responses/month + 50 contract analyses/month.

### Are these the right gates?

**Defense responses: wrong metric to limit.** 150 responses per month is so far above any realistic freelancer usage that it is functionally unlimited. A freelancer handling 10–15 active clients simultaneously might generate 20–30 conflict situations per month at peak stress. At 150, the ceiling is invisible. This means the "150 responses/month" line on the pricing card communicates abundance, not scarcity — which is fine for conversion, but it is not a meaningful value metric.

The risk: if Pro users never approach the limit, the per-response count becomes noise on the pricing page and in the user's mental model of what they are paying for.

**Contract analyses: closer to the right metric.** 50 contract analyses per month is still very generous, but contract uploads are a deliberate action with clear business value (reviewing a new client agreement). This metric maps more directly to a real behavior and a real decision moment.

**What the actual value metric is.** Based on the product's positioning, the true value differentiators between Free and Pro are:
- Full history and response tracking
- Document generation (SOW amendments, kill fee invoices, dispute packages)
- Client notes and project-level context
- Analytics (usage patterns, payment recovery rate)
- Access to all 21 tools without per-use anxiety

These are feature-gated differences, not quantity-gated. The current usage numbers (150/50) are a legacy of typical SaaS freemium convention, but they do not reflect where Pushback's actual value concentration is.

### Recommendation: reframe limits as a safety net, not the pitch

Keep the numeric limits on the pricing page for completeness and to communicate "you won't run out," but do not lead with them as the primary Pro differentiator. The lead differentiators should be:

1. Full history and response tracking (you can see what you sent and when)
2. Document generation (the moment the situation escalates beyond an email)
3. Client intelligence (risk scoring in roadmap — once live, this becomes the clearest Pro anchor)
4. Unlimited contract analyses (50 is fine, but "unlimited" would be stronger)

If the per-response limit is this invisible in practice, consider restructuring as unlimited responses for Pro and simply citing "no cap" rather than a number. It converts better and is honest.

## 4. Alternative Pricing Models Worth Considering

### A. Annual discount (strong — implement immediately)

**Why:** Annual billing addresses the insurance-product psychology problem directly. A freelancer who commits to a year has already resolved the "I only need this when something goes wrong" objection. Annual framing repositions Pushback as infrastructure, not a reactive purchase.

**Suggested pricing:** €180/year (€15/month equivalent — 25% discount). The saving of €60 over monthly is large enough to feel meaningful; the absolute number (€180) remains below the psychological threshold for a considered purchase.

**Positioning copy:** "€180/year — one scope creep situation paid for the year." Or: "€180 once a year. One recovered invoice covers it."

**Display approach:** On the pricing page, show the monthly toggle but default to annual for the Pro card, with the monthly price crossed out or shown as "billed €180/year — save €60."

### B. Per-project pricing (decline — wrong fit)

A per-project model (e.g., €9 to unlock all tools for one project) might seem aligned with freelancer workflows, but it creates the wrong incentives:

- It caps LTV at the single transaction level
- It attracts one-time users who churn immediately
- It undercuts the subscription logic that funds recurring development
- It frames Pushback as a one-off tool, not a standing business toolkit

The product's arsenal positioning requires a subscription to make sense. Per-project is a race to the bottom and destroys retention.

### C. Team/agency tier (future — not now)

An agency tier (€50–75/month for 3–5 seats) makes sense when:
- The product has validated solo freelancer retention
- There is inbound from boutique studios and small agencies
- Collaboration features exist (shared project views, team response history)

Currently, the anti-persona section correctly excludes agencies with internal resources. However, a 2–3 person studio without a dedicated account manager is a natural growth segment. A "Studio" plan at €49/month for up to 3 seats is worth planning for Phase 14+, not now.

### D. Lifetime deal (avoid)

Lifetime deals are appropriate for early traction among a deal-hunting audience (AppSumo, etc.) but are structurally incompatible with a recurring AI inference cost model. If contract analysis and response generation carry real API costs per use, lifetime deals create an unsustainable cost structure as usage scales. Do not pursue.

### E. Usage-based overage pricing (decline for now)

Charging for responses beyond a monthly cap adds billing complexity and anxiety. For a product that promises to remove anxiety from professional situations, a billing model that introduces per-use costs for the exact moments of crisis is psychologically misaligned. Keep pricing flat.

## 5. Upgrade Trigger Optimization

### Current state

The upgrade CTA appears at the limit hit point (`?upgrade=1` query param) and on the dashboard via `UpgradePrompt` when `responsesUsed` is near the free cap. The pricing page is accessible from the landing page and the navbar.

### The problem with limit-only triggers

Waiting for a user to hit the free cap is reactive. A user who has used their 1 free response resolved their immediate situation and is no longer in pain. If the upgrade prompt appears days or weeks later when they return, they have lost the emotional urgency that would have driven the conversion.

### Priority upgrade trigger moments

**1. Immediately after the first response is generated (highest conversion moment)**

The user has just seen their copy-ready message. They are in the highest-confidence moment with the product. This is when to show the upgrade — not when they try to generate a second response and hit a wall.

Implementation: After clicking "Copy message" or marking "Sent" on the first response, show a non-blocking bottom-of-screen banner or inline card: "That's your free response. Upgrade to Pro to handle every situation that follows — €20/month, cancel anytime."

This converts the moment of delight, not the moment of frustration.

**2. Contract analysis result screen**

When the contract analysis returns a risk flag — especially a high-risk clause — the user is in a discovery-of-risk moment. Framing the upgrade as "get the full arsenal to respond to what your contract doesn't protect you from" is directly relevant.

**3. Returning after the free response is consumed**

When a user returns to the app and attempts to open a tool or start a new situation, they hit the limit. This is the expected trigger, but the messaging must be crafted carefully: do not show a generic "you've reached your limit" gate. Show: "Your one free response was sent [X days ago]. Here's what you're prepared for the next time a situation hits — Pro gives you 150 responses and full history."

**4. After adding a second project**

A user who has added two client projects to Pushback is signaling active use intent. This is a behavioral trigger that predicts upgrade readiness. Show a subtle nudge: "You're tracking 2 projects. Pro gives you full history, client notes, and document generation — €20/month."

**5. During project setup when risk factors are high**

If a user creates a project with a payment due date within 7 days, or marks an invoice as overdue during onboarding, show the upgrade CTA in context: "Overdue payments are the most common reason freelancers upgrade. Get the full payment escalation sequence — first reminder through final demand — in Pro."

**6. The "Needs Attention" dashboard widget**

When attention alerts appear (overdue payment, ghost client, stalled project), a Pro CTA framing document generation is highly relevant: "Generate a formal demand letter or dispute package — Pro only." This should appear inline with the alert, not as a separate banner.

## 6. Pricing Page Copy Recommendations

### Current state (landing page pricing section)

**Headline:** "Start free. Own every situation."
**Subheadline:** "Run your first situation free. No card, no friction. Upgrade when the work gets serious."
**Free CTA:** "Run a free situation"
**Pro CTA:** "Get full access"
**Footer note:** "Your contract is never stored after analysis. We read it, flag the risks, delete it."

### Issues

- The headline conflates two tiers. "Own every situation" implies Pro access; "Start free" implies Free. Users read the headline holistically and get mixed signals.
- "Upgrade when the work gets serious" implies that the free tier is sufficient for casual situations and Pro is only for serious ones. This undersells the persistent value of Pro for any active freelancer.
- "Get full access" is generic. It does not communicate what access delivers in business terms.
- The free CTA "Run a free situation" is good — it is action-oriented and specific.
- The privacy footer note is correctly placed and should remain.

### Recommended copy

**Pricing section label (eyebrow):** PRICING

**Headline:** "Start with your first situation. Never face another one unprepared."

**Subheadline:** "Try the defense response for free — no card. When the next situation hits, you'll already have it open."

**Free tier headline:** Starter

**Free tier subheadline:** Your first situation

**Free features (reordered by impact):**
- 1 defense response — copy-ready in under 30 seconds
- 1 contract risk analysis
- Access to all 21 defense tools
- No card required

**Free CTA:** "Handle your first situation free"

**Pro tier headline:** Pro

**Pro tier subheadline:** Every situation, handled

**Pro features (reordered by impact):**
- Unlimited defense responses
- 50 contract risk analyses/month
- Full response history — see what you sent and when
- Document generation — SOW amendments, kill fee invoices, dispute packages
- Project-level client notes
- Usage analytics and payment recovery tracking
- All 21 defense tools, fully deployed

**Pro CTA:** "Get the full arsenal"

**Below the Pro card (urgency anchor):** "One recovered invoice covers a year of Pro."

**Below the pricing grid (trust + objection):** "Your contract is never stored. We read it, flag the risks, delete it. Cancel Pro anytime."

### Pricing page structural improvement

Add a simple ROI calculator or static callout below the pricing grid:

> Freelancer billing at €80/hr. One hour of scope creep deflected = €80.
> Pro costs €20/month. That's one situation per quarter.

This is not a calculator with inputs — just a static, credible framing that does the math for the most common freelancer hourly rate. It pre-empts the "€20 is a lot" objection with math rather than copy.
